# NYHA Functional Classification of Heart Failure

The New York Heart Association (NYHA) functional classification system categorizes heart failure severity based on patient symptoms during physical activity. It is the most widely used system for describing heart failure severity and guides treatment decisions.

## Class I — No Limitation
Ordinary physical activity does not cause undue fatigue, palpitation, dyspnea, or anginal pain. Patients can perform activities such as walking, climbing stairs, and carrying groceries without symptoms. Ejection fraction may be reduced but the patient is asymptomatic.

## Class II — Slight Limitation
Comfortable at rest. Ordinary physical activity such as walking more than two blocks on level ground or climbing more than one flight of stairs results in fatigue, palpitation, dyspnea, or anginal pain. Patients typically have an ejection fraction of 30-40% but can range widely.

## Class III — Marked Limitation
Comfortable at rest. Less than ordinary physical activity causes fatigue, palpitation, dyspnea, or anginal pain. Activities such as dressing, walking across the room, or minimal household tasks produce symptoms. Patients are typically managed with aggressive GDMT and are candidates for device therapy (ICD, CRT). CardioMEMS implantation is indicated for NYHA Class III patients with a prior heart failure hospitalization.

## Class IV — Unable to Carry On Any Physical Activity Without Discomfort
Symptoms of heart failure are present at rest. Any physical activity increases discomfort. Patients are often bedbound or chair-bound. May require inotropic support, mechanical circulatory support (LVAD), or heart transplant evaluation.

## Clinical Significance
- NYHA class can change over time with treatment optimization
- Class III is the most common indication for advanced heart failure therapies
- CardioMEMS FDA expanded indication (February 2022) now includes NYHA Class II patients with elevated natriuretic peptides (BNP ≥ 250 pg/mL or NT-proBNP ≥ 1000 pg/mL), adding approximately 1.2 million eligible patients in the United States
- Transition from Class II to Class III is a critical inflection point for treatment escalation
